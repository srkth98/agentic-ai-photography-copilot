# Exposure Triangle Guide

## Purpose

This guide explains the three core camera settings — ISO, aperture, and shutter
speed — that together control exposure, and how to balance them for different
shooting conditions.

## The Three Pillars

### Aperture (f-stop)

Aperture controls how much light enters through the lens and affects depth of field.

* Low f-numbers (f/1.4, f/1.8, f/2.8) = wide opening, more light, shallow depth of
  field (blurry background).
* High f-numbers (f/8, f/11, f/16) = narrow opening, less light, deeper depth of
  field (more in focus).

Use a wide aperture for portraits and low-light shots. Use a narrow aperture for
landscapes where you want everything sharp.

### Shutter Speed

Shutter speed controls how long the sensor is exposed to light and affects motion.

* Fast shutter speeds (1/500s and faster) freeze motion — good for sports and
  wildlife.
* Slow shutter speeds (1/30s and slower) create motion blur — good for light
  trails, waterfalls, and panning shots.
* Below about 1/60s handheld, camera shake becomes a common cause of blur. Use a
  tripod or image stabilization for slow shutter speeds.

### ISO

ISO controls the sensor's sensitivity to light.

* Low ISO (100-400) = cleaner images with less noise, best in bright conditions.
* High ISO (800-6400+) = brighter images in low light, but introduces visible
  grain/noise.

## Balancing the Triangle

Exposure is a balance: increasing one setting to brighten the image generally
means decreasing another to compensate.

| Scenario | Aperture | Shutter Speed | ISO |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bright outdoor daylight | f/8-f/11 | 1/250s | 100 |
| Indoor available light | f/1.8-f/2.8 | 1/60-1/125s | 800-3200 |
| Low light handheld | f/1.4-f/2.8 | 1/60s minimum | 1600-6400 |
| Night with tripod | f/8-f/16 | 1-30s | 100-800 |

## Common Issue: Blurry Photos Indoors / At Night

Symptoms:

* Photos look soft, smeared, or "double exposed"
* Subjects appear sharp in bright light but blurry indoors

Resolution:

1. Check your shutter speed — if it's below 1/60s while handheld, motion blur from
   hand shake is the likely cause.
2. Widen your aperture (lower f-number) to let in more light without slowing the
   shutter further.
3. Raise the ISO to 800-3200 to allow a faster shutter speed in dim conditions.
4. Use a tripod or brace the camera on a stable surface for shutter speeds slower
   than 1/30s.
5. Enable image stabilization (IS/VR/OSS) on your lens or camera body if available.
6. If the subject itself is moving, prioritize a faster shutter speed (1/125s or
   faster) and compensate with a wider aperture and higher ISO.

## Escalation Criteria

Seek further help if:

* Photos remain blurry even at fast shutter speeds and proper technique — this may
  indicate an autofocus or lens calibration issue (see troubleshooting guide).
* Noise at moderate ISO (800) is excessive — sensor or firmware issue, contact
  support.
