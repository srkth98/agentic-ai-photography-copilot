# Photography Genres & Settings Guide

## Purpose

This guide provides starting-point camera settings and tips for common
photography genres: portrait, landscape, sports, wildlife, astro, macro, night,
and street photography.

## Portrait Photography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/1.8-f/2.8, Shutter 1/200s or faster, ISO 100-400
* Use a wide aperture to create background blur (bokeh) that isolates the
  subject.
* Focus on the eyes — eyes should be the sharpest point in the frame.
* Soft, diffused light (overcast days, golden hour, or bounced flash) is most
  flattering.

## Landscape Photography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/8-f/11, Shutter 1/125s (or longer on tripod), ISO 100-200
* Use a narrow aperture for greater depth of field — keeping foreground and
  background sharp.
* Use a tripod for sharp images at low ISO, especially in low light (sunrise/
  sunset).
* Consider graduated filters or HDR/exposure blending for high-contrast scenes
  (bright sky, dark foreground).

## Sports Photography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/2.8-f/4, Shutter 1/1000s or faster, ISO 400-1600
* Use continuous/burst shooting mode and continuous autofocus (AF-C/AI Servo) to
  track moving subjects.
* A fast shutter speed freezes action; panning (matching camera movement to
  subject movement at a slower shutter speed) creates motion blur in the
  background while keeping the subject sharp.

## Wildlife Photography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/4-f/5.6, Shutter 1/1000s or faster, ISO 400-1600
* Use a telephoto lens (200mm+) and continuous autofocus.
* Shoot in burst mode to capture the best moment (eye contact, wing position,
  etc.).
* Be patient and use natural cover/blinds to avoid disturbing animals.

## Astrophotography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/1.4-f/2.8, Shutter 15-25s, ISO 1600-3200
* Use a sturdy tripod — long exposures require a completely stationary camera.
* The "500 rule" (or "NPF rule" for more precision): divide 500 by your focal
  length (accounting for crop factor) to estimate the maximum shutter speed before
  star trails become visible (e.g. 500/20mm = 25 seconds).
* Use manual focus set to infinity, verified with live view zoom on a bright star.
* Shoot in a dark location away from light pollution for best results.

## Macro Photography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/8-f/16, Shutter 1/200s, ISO 200-800
* Use a dedicated macro lens for high magnification (1:1 or greater).
* Narrow apertures help compensate for the extremely shallow depth of field at
  close focusing distances.
* A tripod, focus rail, or focus stacking technique (combining multiple images
  focused at different depths) can dramatically increase sharpness across the
  subject.

## Night Photography (Cityscapes, Light Trails)

* **Settings**: Aperture f/1.8-f/2.8 (handheld) or f/8-f/16 (tripod, light trails),
  Shutter 1/30-2s, ISO 800-3200
* For light trails (e.g. car traffic), use a tripod, narrow aperture, and longer
  shutter speeds (several seconds).
* For handheld night shots, prioritize a wide aperture and higher ISO to keep
  shutter speeds fast enough to avoid blur.

## Street Photography

* **Settings**: Aperture f/5.6-f/8, Shutter 1/250s, ISO 200-800
* Use a moderate wide-angle or standard prime lens (28-50mm) for a natural
  perspective and quick framing.
* Zone focusing (pre-setting focus distance and aperture for sufficient depth of
  field) helps capture candid moments quickly without waiting for autofocus.

## Choosing the Right Genre Settings

If you're unsure which genre best matches your scenario, describe the subject and
lighting conditions — the assistant can recommend a starting preset, which the
gear lookup tool can supply directly via the settings recommendation tool.

## Escalation Criteria

These are starting points, not fixed rules — adjust based on your specific
lighting, subject movement, and creative intent. If your camera consistently
produces unexpected exposures despite following these settings, check for sensor
or metering issues in the troubleshooting guide.
