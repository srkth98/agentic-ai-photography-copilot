# Composition Techniques Guide

## Purpose

This guide covers fundamental composition principles that improve the visual
impact of a photograph, independent of camera settings or gear.

## Rule of Thirds

Divide the frame into a 3x3 grid. Place key subjects or horizon lines along the
grid lines or at their intersections rather than dead center. Most cameras can
display this grid in the viewfinder or live view — enable it under display
settings.

## Leading Lines

Use natural lines (roads, fences, rivers, architecture) to draw the viewer's eye
toward the main subject. Diagonal lines tend to feel more dynamic than perfectly
horizontal or vertical ones.

## Framing

Use foreground elements (doorways, branches, arches) to create a "frame within a
frame" around the subject, adding depth and context.

## Symmetry and Patterns

Symmetrical scenes (reflections, architecture) and repeating patterns are visually
satisfying. Deliberately breaking a pattern with one different element can create
a strong focal point.

## Depth and Layering

Include foreground, midground, and background elements to create a sense of
depth, especially in landscape photography. A wide aperture (low f-number) can
isolate a subject from a busy background by blurring it (shallow depth of field).

## Negative Space

Leaving empty space (sky, plain walls, water) around a subject can emphasize
isolation, scale, or simplicity. Don't feel obligated to fill the entire frame.

## Genre-Specific Composition Tips

### Portraits

* Position eyes near the upper third of the frame.
* Leave space in the direction the subject is looking ("looking room").
* Use a clean or blurred background to avoid distraction.

### Landscapes

* Place the horizon along the upper or lower third, not the center, unless
  symmetry is the intent (e.g. reflections).
* Include a foreground element to add depth.
* Use leading lines (rivers, paths) to guide the eye toward the background.

### Street Photography

* Anticipate moments — pre-focus on a spot and wait for a subject to enter the
  frame.
* Use environmental context (signs, shadows, reflections) to tell a story.

## Common Issue: Photos Feel "Flat" or Unbalanced

Symptoms:

* The subject is technically sharp and well-exposed but the image feels boring or
  cluttered

Resolution:

1. Check whether the subject is dead-center with no intentional symmetry — try
   recomposing using the rule of thirds.
2. Look for distracting background elements (poles, bright spots, people) and
   reposition or use a wider aperture to blur them out.
3. Add a foreground element for depth, especially in wide shots.
4. Try shooting from a different height (crouch down or get higher) for a fresh
   perspective.
5. Simplify — remove or crop out elements that don't support the main subject.

## Escalation Criteria

Composition issues are stylistic, not technical — there is no "escalation" beyond
practice and critique. However, if a perceived composition problem is actually
caused by a tilted horizon or lens distortion on every shot, check the
lens/troubleshooting guide for a hardware-related cause.
