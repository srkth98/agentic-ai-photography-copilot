# Lighting Techniques Guide

## Purpose

This guide covers natural light, artificial light, and flash techniques for
achieving well-exposed, flattering photographs in a variety of conditions.

## Natural Light

### Golden Hour

The hour after sunrise and before sunset produces warm, soft, directional light —
ideal for portraits and landscapes.

* Position the subject so the light comes from the side or slightly behind for a
  rim-light effect.
* Use a reflector or fill flash to brighten shadows on the subject's face if the
  sun is behind them.

### Overcast / Cloudy Days

Clouds act as a giant diffuser, producing soft, even light with minimal harsh
shadows — excellent for portraits and macro work.

### Harsh Midday Sun

Direct overhead sun creates strong shadows under eyes and noses.

* Move the subject into open shade (under a tree, awning, or doorway).
* Use a diffuser between the sun and the subject.
* Use fill flash or a reflector to soften shadows.

## Artificial / Studio Lighting

### One-Light Setup

A single light source (flash, strobe, or continuous light) positioned at roughly
45 degrees to the subject and slightly above eye level creates classic, flattering
portrait lighting.

### Three-Point Lighting

* **Key light**: Main light source, typically at 45 degrees to the subject.
* **Fill light**: Softer, opposite the key light, fills in shadows.
* **Back/rim light**: Behind the subject, separates them from the background.

## On-Camera Flash Techniques

### Issue: Harsh, Flat-Looking Flash Photos

Symptoms:

* Subject looks washed out with a harsh shadow behind them
* Background is very dark compared to the subject

Resolution:

1. Bounce the flash off a white ceiling or wall instead of firing it directly at
   the subject (if your flash head tilts/swivels).
2. Reduce flash power (use flash exposure compensation, e.g. -1 to -2 EV) and let
   ambient light contribute more.
3. Move the subject away from walls to avoid hard background shadows.
4. Use a diffuser or bounce card attached to the flash head.
5. Slow the shutter speed slightly (within flash sync limits, typically up to
   1/200s) to let more ambient light into the frame and balance the background.

## Indoor Low-Light Shooting

* Increase ISO (800-3200) before resorting to flash if you want to preserve
  ambient mood.
* Use a wide-aperture lens (f/1.8 or wider) to maximize available light.
* Use continuous (LED) lights for video and for previewing how light falls before
  taking a still.

## Escalation Criteria

* If a flash unit fails to fire, misfires randomly, or overheats quickly, stop use
  and have it inspected — this can indicate a capacitor or trigger circuit fault.
* If consistent color casts appear in images regardless of white balance setting,
  check for mixed lighting sources (e.g. tungsten + daylight) — consider gels to
  match color temperatures.
